The Math Whale Project is an experimental software exercise that intends to mix the colors green and blue to form a sea green effect. What pain discounts itself as is examinated with various means to track and re-route personal journeys to better outcomes. Our different sections highlight specs of our project in great detail to get you onboarded with these conceptual pieces of text.

Disclaimer 1: Ages 21 and above only.

Everyone experiences pain in different measures which can not be compared to another. What we find is inherently common though, are the ways we deal with the different manifestations of pain, and how we can positively or even negatively utilise them to live with a kinder state of mind, towards ourselves, our immedidiate circles of friends, family, and strangers.

To be more perceptive to the other, sea deeper into how we find meaning in occurances, why we take the decisions we take, to hopefully then become more mindful of our participation in the consumption of resources available to us at any given time, and save the planet, and ourselves, and all the big things, for the n-th time.

Disclaimer 2: Warning.

It is a risk to reference this project off of the blue whale challenge that took place in 2016 in various countries. To summarise, it 'targeted' the weaker, more emotional aspects within our society, by asking younger groups of individuals to play, who were more susceptible to experiencing pain in drastic senses within the gray areas of seeming irrationalities.

We cap our age limit to adults here because this project is designed to rework our perspectives towards others in a more realised state of durability of each other. As more neutral versions of ourselves, we can identify the shortcomings, the peak points, and complexity of human behavior through the lens of listed breakpoint suggestions.

To identify would be the first step to being aware of spectrums that exist in each of us, dormant or not. This step should then be necessarily followed by perceptibility to emotions within ourselves to the limits of understanding empathy in all its degrees. Its found that the second step is the most time and attention consuming task for all subjects, the ones experiencing pain as well as the ones studying it. While a child experiences pain in a more elaborate sense, as adults we feel equally at distant with our emotions at times. Not to suggest that some feel more than others, but just like in any cyclic chain of events, the ones who are subjected to seeking knowledge more predominantly, on the path of pain, tend to also find it with the help of perspectives, opening doorways to the ones who are yet to be in similar en-routes.

It is easy to disregard the journeys some people are on, in the name of humanity as a whole, to keep mentalities safe and all cherry. Nobody likes to see the darkness that presents itself for what it is, least of all the people who aren't forcefully subjected to it. The helpless ones either subdue their outlooks on life to be accepted in a working society, or they give up on connecting with the ones who've had it easy. This choice of outcomes only harms all by increasing mistrust in each other, and giving way to a more isolated way of being. This grey zone of interactions is also where all of the answers lie to the megaproblems of society. To save the one percent of us who do find themselves in the dark vague is a necessary suit that should be crafted rightfully so, for the sake of everyone.

To use a metaphor, like with any machine, if any of the parts aren't functional, the entire system suffers in some way. And it's only a matter of time until chance decides which parts get the junk on them. The only limitation of this metaphor, that in turn completes it in this instance, is that instead of having the option to throw away the parts, we need to learn to clean them. Ourselves. For others to realise those perspectives, learn something from the happened, and apply it towards their own understanding of others, can save everyone a whole bracket of time and expenses, and funnily so, pain and suffering.

Pain is an inherent part of living, that drapes itself in a variety of other forms. It is a necessary tool for existing. No one likes it, and everyone has to deal with it.
